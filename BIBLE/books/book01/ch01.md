# Chapter 1 — The Shatter Night

# 

# London glittered like a spell that didn’t know it was about to break.

# 

# Banners hung from the South Bank in Ministry-approved colors, tasteful charms keeping them aloft against the winter wind. Magic—sanctioned, celebratory—rolled over the crowds in soft pulses: warming charms for Muggle onlookers, anti-slick hexes on the paving stones, an innocuous rinsing breeze that carried the scent of sugar and smoke from paper cones of roasted almonds. The Ministry had framed it as closure. Voldemort gone, trials concluded, the new Minister sworn in. A final exhibition—heroes and fireworks and a curated future.

# 

# Harry stood in the wings of the temporary stage with his cloak tight around him and tried not to feel like he was being displayed.

# 

# “Smile,” Hermione murmured at his shoulder. “Or at least soften the jaw before you crack a molar.”

# 

# He unclenched. “Does this… help?” He arranged something that might pass for polite.

# 

# “Marginally.” She adjusted the collar of his cloak like she was tidying a parchment. “Then we go home. Kingsley wants this to be the last spectacle. You give them the shape of an ending, and we… we get on with the work.”

# 

# “‘The shape of an ending’ sounds like a curse,” Harry said, but he nodded. Truth was, he could already feel the charge in the city, ambient and eager. Every light on the Embankment flickered in rhythm with the river’s current, and the wind made a bell-glass hum along the balustrade whenever it gusted just right. His magic pricked at his skin in accompaniment, like a tiger pacing behind ribs.

# 

# Hermione followed his gaze across the water. “Muggles everywhere,” she said softly. “Phones out. Careful, all right? No duels. A flourish, a bow, and wave like you’re not planning to vanish as soon as—”

# 

# “I promise,” he said, not lying so much as hoping hard.

# 

# They called him to the stage with a voice amplified by charm and microphone both. He walked out to a roar that was part cheers, part recorded music swelling on cue. Cameras—Muggle and wizard—tracked him with red eyes.

# 

# It would have been easy to simply levitate a lattice of lights and form them into a stag. He could have conjured an auroral curtain and let it drift over the river. He knew the Ministry’s script: the Boy Who Lived gives a final, gentle bow to a grateful world.

# 

# He lifted his wand. The Elder wand thrummed like an instrument tuning for a note only he could hear.

# 

# “Harry,” Hermione hissed from the wing, low and urgent. “Small.”

# 

# He nodded, and then the night changed.

# 

# There are moments when power stops pretending it is something you hold and reminds you that it is something that holds you.

# 

# He didn’t mean to pull from his chest instead of the wand. It happened when his breath hit the cold just wrong and the light along the Thames struck the cut in his palm where a cup had broken that morning. Intent, not words. The Aureate Core woke and answered.

# 

# Everything made of glass began to sing.

# 

# Every telephone booth on the Embankment, every pane in every riverside restaurant, the little rounded bulbs in tourist fairy lights: a clear, faint chiming as if the city had caught its breath in crystal. The sound found a harmonic in Harry’s bones and resonated until the world went quietly, mercilessly sharp.

# 

# He tried to close his hand around the magic and discovered his hand was already a fist. He tried to lower his wand and found he had already lowered it. The pressure in his chest continued to bloom, golden and terrible, ahead of his orders.

# 

# “Stop,” he told it, and realized he’d not used a word at all.

# 

# Light unfurled over the river like a sail turned inside out. An aureate hemisphere flowered from the center of the Thames at Waterloo Bridge, reached up and over, and for one impossible second the bridge itself appeared to bend. The water flattened under the weight of the shield, then rose against it in a shimmering dome of displaced midnight.

# 

# People screamed. They thought it was part of the show. They kept screaming when the first hairline crack stitched itself along the stone. The glass-voice everywhere modulated from bell to kettle, rising and rising.

# 

# Harry shifted his footing without thinking. The shield belonged to him; the collapse would, too. He pushed his palm toward the bridge and felt the choke of physics against spellcraft, mass and mortar and City-of-London history dragging on his will like seaweed on an ankle. Somewhere, Hermione cursed with perfect enunciation.

# 

# It wasn’t skill, not exactly. It was decision. He decided the stress should go somewhere else. He decided weight was sand. He decided the water could take it. The bridge’s cracking went sideways instead of down; stone slid with a whisper instead of a roar; the aureate dome flexed—then thinned—then bowed, and the river spat back what he asked of it.

# 

# A bus teetered. Someone clung to a railing by their fingertips and looked at him like a person looks at the god that has decided to exist.

# 

# Phones, a forest of them, recorded every moment.

# 

# “Harry!” Hermione again, nearer now, cutting through his focus. “Enough!”

# 

# He bled the light down as if unscrewing a jar lid. The dome sagged and broke, not into shards but into rain; the river took back the river. The bridge held, a hair thinner, a little wounded, still itself. The sound of glass shivering drifted away down alleys and up into the winter stars, and London exhaled the way a crowd does when a coin toss comes up life.

# 

# He realized he was already standing on the rim of the Embankment rail.

# 

# “How—” Hermione’s breath steamed beside him. “You can’t put a city under a bell jar, Harry. You cannot. That’s not—”

# 

# “I didn’t,” he said, and only then noticed his hands were shaking. Not with adrenaline. With a fine after-vibration, like strings that would not stop humming. He had done too much, too fast, without asking the part of him that kept the ledger. A quiet ache notched behind his right eye. Soul-Scorch: the little splinters he accumulated when he spent more of himself than he should. He reached by habit for an Anchor—the memory of Hagrid’s laugh in his hut’s doorway—and found it like a handhold in a storm.

# 

# Security witches in ceremonial uniforms formed a screen around the stage as if they could block cameras with fabric. Kingsley crossed the boards with the grave pace of a man who understood that what had not happened mattered less than what had been seen.

# 

# He stopped an arm’s length away. “We were meant to demonstrate control,” he said without heat, voice pitched so the nearest microphone would catch something reasonable. “Thank you for preventing catastrophe.”

# 

# Harry could feel the drag of the thread between Minister and meaning. Thank you, and also: what have you become, and also: how do I govern you.

# 

# He said, “I’m sorry.”

# 

# “You’ve apologised to half the world in the last year.” Kingsley’s eyes ticked toward the opposite bank where the dense constellation of phone screens shone. “Not tonight. We’ll spin it.” Then, quieter, for Harry alone: “Get off the stage before you’re asked questions you won’t answer kindly.”

# 

# He went. Hermione went with him. They left the roar and the bulbs and the brass behind and found the narrower dark under the arches where the city keeps its damp. Hermione’s shoes clicked on old stone.

# 

# “You need to sleep,” she said, which was code for everything else. “And you need to listen when I tell you the difference between what you can do and what you should.”

# 

# He didn’t argue. He didn’t agree. His magic felt like it had taken a bite out of him and was politely waiting to see if he noticed.

# 

# They were three arches along when the first siren went up from the Muggle side.

# 

# Harry paused. The sound should have been ridiculous—Muggle noise, red lights, mundane consequence—but it threaded neatly into the last of the glass-bell hum and tied a bow between worlds. Hermione glanced over her shoulder, expression shuttered.

# 

# “Phones,” she said. “There are a hundred angles. A thousand. Harry, we were sloppy—no, I’m sorry, you were generous in a way the world has to be ready for, and it isn’t, not yet. We need to—”

# 

# A chiming came from her jacket pocket. She fished out a small slate of lacquered wood studded with runes that glowed to spell out the letters of Kingsley’s name. She pressed her thumb to the top. “Yes. We’re clear. No, he’s… he’s all right.” She listened, paled a degree. “Yes, sir.”

# 

# She ended the call and said, “Fifteen minutes ago, a BBC journalist asked on-air if this was a government-sanctioned display. They have an expert—some professor—saying the light was ‘coherent plasma.’ The Ministry’s Muggle Liaison is already at Scotland Yard. There will be questions Parliament wants to ask tonight.” Her breath clouded the arch air. “Harry, you can’t go home.”

# 

# “Good,” he said before he thought about the word. “Home isn’t… restful at the moment.”

# 

# “Ginny—” Hermione began.

# 

# He swallowed around the shape of her name. An Anchor when held gently, a blade when mishandled. “Tonight I’ll make a mistake if I see anyone I love.”

# 

# For once, Hermione didn’t argue. “Then we do the next right thing. We get above the city. We make a plan that assumes every second person with a camera is our enemy and every third is an ally. We put something on the record that isn’t panic.”

# 

# They cut across the Embankment under a charm that made attention slide off them like rain off oilcloth, Hermione steering with the efficiency of someone who had built a map of London out of books. Sirens yodeled. On the water, a patrol boat cut a white wound and stitched it back up with wake.

# 

# On a narrow set of iron steps that felt like they’d been installed in the reign of some pragmatic Roman, they climbed to a service ledge beneath a bridge. The river smell was stronger there—metal and algae and cold. Harry leaned on the rail and looked at the water where his light had been.

# 

# He knew, in the way a deep indrawn breath knows its exhale, that something had shifted. He could count the number of times he’d been filmed doing anything more magical than a lumos since the war on one hand, and most of those had involved Ministry camerawitches who correct the truth into palatability. This had been something else. The city had watched a god test his range and decide to be merciful.

# 

# From the shadows at the edge of the ledge, a voice said, “You always did have taste for spectacle.”

# 

# Harry turned, wand sliding into his palm with the old friend’s certainty that nothing else in his life had managed. The man who stepped into the light wasn’t a man he recognized, which meant he recognized him immediately: too clean to be a vagrant, too alert to be merely curious, the kind of deliberate posture you learn after you train to survive.

# 

# He had no wand Harry could see. He had a black case slung under one arm and a smile that practiced at being disarming.

# 

# “I’ll be quick,” the stranger said. His accent was London by way of Sandhurst, words clipped into manageable shapes. “A pleasure—and I mean that sincerely—to meet you, Mr Potter. You’ve just made my department’s night both easier and more complicated.”

# 

# “Your department?” Hermione stood at a little diagonal to Harry’s left, where she could step into or away from a hex without thinking about it. “Name, please, and affiliation.”

# 

# “Samuel Price,” he said. “Taskforce Janus.” He shrugged, apology and humor. “You weren’t told about us. That’s rather the point.”

# 

# “Wizards don’t answer to Muggle taskforces,” Harry said, smoke without flame.

# 

# “Wizards don’t answer to anyone if they don’t want to,” Price agreed. “That’s what keeps me employed.”

# 

# He held up his free hand, palm open, showing a small disk the size of a sickle. It glittered with little etched lines like a spider had tried to become a cartographer. Harry felt the faintest itch along the surface of his magic, a distortion around the edges of his focus, like a reflection disturbed in a bowl. The disk gave off a hush where no magic wanted to settle.

# 

# “RF mesh prototype,” Price said conversationally. “We’ve been playing with signal patterns to disrupt—what do your textbooks call it—spatial harmonics? Awful name, but useful. Point is, I doubt it would stop you. It might slow down anyone trying to turn my team into a soup.”

# 

# “You brought it to a conversation,” Hermione said, “which is either brave or unwise.”

# 

# “It’s insurance,” Price said, unruffled. “The kind that pays out to everyone if nobody uses it.”

# 

# Harry could have taken it out of the man’s hand with a thought. He didn’t. He’d already bent one piece of London tonight. He was trying very hard not to learn how people broke.

# 

# “What do you want?” he asked.

# 

# “To keep the lights on,” Price said simply. “My brief is to prevent panic. That looks, in policy memos, like ‘containing anomalous events’ and ‘maintaining public order.’ In practice it looks like standing under bridges and asking you not to do that again over Westminster while the Six O’Clock News is live.”

# 

# “Would you like me to go back in time and not be born?” Harry said, and the flatness in his voice made Hermione flick her eyes toward him sharply. He modulated his tone. “People were falling. I caught a bridge.”

# 

# “And we thank you,” Price said. “We also have five hundred videos of what you did and a nation that will think its government is hiding aliens. Help me help you. We’re going to put out a line about a ‘light display’ and ‘authorised emergency response drill.’ It won’t hold long. It buys us hours. If you stand up and give us a sentence that doesn’t make you sound like a myth, we might make it to morning without Parliament walking out of bed in its slippers and into a security committee.”

# 

# Hermione touched Harry’s elbow. “He’s right about the hours. He’s wrong about what sits on the other side of them.”

# 

# Price’s eyes were not unkind. “On the other side is a conversation about how we co-exist without the underside of London being paved in… whatever you’re made of.”

# 

# Harry should have said no. He should have walked away and left the Ministry to their fiddles. But he could feel the river slow under the arch and the weight of all those cameras compressing into a single point in time the way gravity draws knots in space. The decision wasn’t about whether to be seen. It was about whether to choose what being seen would mean.

# 

# “What sentence,” he said, “would keep your committee in its slippers?”

# 

# Price considered, then offered, “No one is in danger, and we will be working with your government to ensure that remains true.”

# 

# “That,” Hermione said, “is not a sentence I can endorse without a translator.”

# 

# “Tell them the truth,” Harry said before she could begin to iterate caveats. “Tell them I caught a bridge.”

# 

# “That sounds like hubris,” Hermione murmured.

# 

# “It sounds like accountability,” he said. He stepped closer to the lip of the ledge and looked out at the lights again. The cold sharpened his lungs. The ache behind his eye ticked once, twice, then settled like a coin choosing which side it wanted to be. “You get your hours,” he said to Price. “We’ll use them. But understand that I am not… manageable. Not by your radios. Not by your committees. Not even by my friends when I’m tired.”

# 

# Price’s smile thinned into something that respected the weather. “Truth told plainly,” he said. “We’ll see if Parliament prefers it.”

# 

# He slipped the disk back into his pocket and faded into the deeper dark as if he’d rehearsed that trick. Harry listened until the footsteps joined the ordinary noise of the city, then exhaled.

# 

# “You shouldn’t have promised anything,” Hermione said.

# 

# “I promised him hours.” He rubbed his brow with the heel of his hand. “I will give you the morning. After that—”

# 

# “After that,” she said, “we make sure your Anchors are in reach, we eat something that wasn’t conjured, and we draft a statement that can survive contact with reality. I will not let you do this alone, Harry.”

# 

# He looked at her and saw the person who had taught him to read contracts before he signed them, who had written him a list when lists were all that stood between him and falling apart. The ache in his head softened a fraction.

# 

# “Thank you,” he said, and meant it.

# 

# They left the ledge and took the old iron steps down again into the raw air off the Thames. The city’s laughter was brittle, but it was laughter. Across the water, blue strobes etched the outline of the bridge in slow pulses that made the night look like a heartbeat.

# 

# They were almost back under the stage when a ripple went through the crowd that had nothing to do with music. People turned, phones raised. The roar that rose wasn’t joy and wasn’t fear; it was the sound of a thought becoming a fact.

# 

# Harry didn’t have to ask what they were looking at. He could feel it in the prickle along his skin and the way his breath hit the cold: thousands upon thousands of little glass eyes held up to the night, all of them catching what he was. For the first time since he had stepped into this new shape of himself, he felt something like vertigo.

# 

# “Let them see,” he said into the space between one breath and the next. “If they’re going to fear me, let it be for something I actually did.”

# 

# He stepped out from under the arch and into the light.

# 

# Phones lifted as if compelled by a gentle magnetism. Someone shouted his name; someone else asked if this was safe; someone else, in the back, began to cry. He raised a hand without wand or word and gently, deliberately, lifted three shards of glass from the riverside pavement—remnants of some broken stall lamp—and balanced them in a calm triangle in the air.

# 

# No fireworks. No dome. Just a proof: there is a system to this. There is a hand on the lever.

# 

# “My name is Harry Potter,” he said, his voice steady enough to sit on a news broadcast without shaking. “No one is in danger. We will answer your questions in the morning. Tonight, go home.”

# 

# He dropped the shards back to the pavement with a sound like a small bell, and the crowd—lacking a script—took his words as one. A path opened through bodies. He walked it. Hermione matched him step for step. The Ministry’s security swallowed them, and the stage, and the banners that had already become out of place in their own scene.

# 

# On the way into the curtains, Kingsley caught Harry’s sleeve. “You just made every story in this city simpler and harder,” he said. “If I were a poet I’d thank you. I am a politician, so I won’t.”

# 

# “Tomorrow,” Harry said, “we’ll be honest.”

# 

# “Tomorrow,” Kingsley agreed, which was the harshest threat Harry had heard in months.

# 

# They vanished into the apparatus that governments build to keep themselves from learning too much too quickly. Behind them, the river moved on, and the city practiced retelling what it had seen.

# 

# Upstream, in the darkness beneath an arch where the iron steps ended, the last of the aureate light that had clung to stone flickered and went out. The air there held an echo anyway, a faint after-sound, as if a bell that had not been built yet had rung.

# 

# The night remembered.

# 

# And everywhere that a small piece of glass lay in a pocket, or a phone slept with its red eye full of golden light, the world waited for morning.
