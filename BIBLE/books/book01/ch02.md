# Scene A: Westminster Debate

# 

# Hermione Granger had fought battles in dark forests, crumbling castles, and graveyards lit only by curses—but she had never felt so outnumbered as she did standing in the chamber of the House of Commons at three in the morning.

# 

# The air smelled of sweat and dust, the tang of too many bodies forced to wait too long, pressed beneath lamps that flickered with Muggle electricity instead of flame. Every bench was packed with MPs pulled from sleep and driven here by headlines their aides shoved into their hands: BRIDGE OF LIGHT. CITY UNDER A SPELL. BOY WHO LIVED OR DARK LORD REBORN?

# 

# It was supposed to have been a closed session. Instead, the press gallery was overflowing, camera lenses gleaming red like hungry eyes, dictaphones and enchanted quills scratching as fast as ink could flow. Someone had bribed or bullied their way in, and no one had dared clear them out.

# 

# Kingsley Shacklebolt stood at the dispatch box, his hands braced against the carved wood. He looked every inch the Minister for Magic and none of the tired man he must have been; his baritone rolled out into the chamber with the weight of a bell.

# 

# “No lives were lost,” he said for the third time that night. “The situation is under Ministry control.”

# 

# It landed like a stone dropped into a flood—disappearing instantly, overtaken by the current.

# 

# A thin-faced MP with a voice like breaking glass rose from the government benches. “Control? Half my constituents have videos of a boy bending the Thames in half. Do you expect us to accept that this was… fireworks?”

# 

# Murmurs swelled. Phones were raised again, screens replaying the golden dome with Harry’s silhouette at its center. A thousand silent witnesses testifying in unison.

# 

# Kingsley did not flinch. “I expect you to remember that if not for Mr Potter’s intervention, we would be mourning the loss of Waterloo Bridge and the hundreds upon it. What you saw was not an attack. It was a shield. A mistake in scale, perhaps—but an act of protection nonetheless.”

# 

# The MP snorted. “Protection? He nearly snapped the bridge like a breadstick. How long before his mistakes kill instead of save?”

# 

# The murmur turned into something louder. The press quills hissed like snakes.

# 

# Hermione rose from the backbench before she thought better of it. The chamber turned toward her as one—some with recognition, others with suspicion. She was a witch, standing uninvited in the seat of Muggle democracy, and that fact alone made every word matter twice as much.

# 

# “Harry Potter is not your enemy,” she said, and made her voice carry like a spell. “If not for him, the Thames would be filled with the drowned. He caught the bridge. He saved lives.”

# 

# “You admit it was his doing,” a woman from the Defence Committee cut in, sharp as steel. “You admit he conjured this… this weapon.”

# 

# Hermione forced her hands to stay clasped in front of her, not to rise in the familiar rhythm of a wand. “I admit he has power,” she said. “But power is not intent. Harry is reckless, stubborn, sometimes impossible—but he is not malicious. He was trying to save people, and he succeeded.”

# 

# “That makes him less a man and more a sovereign state,” the Defence MP shot back. “And sovereign states are contained or negotiated with. Which do you propose?”

# 

# The word contained hung in the air like a curse.

# 

# Hermione’s mouth went dry. The Statute of Secrecy had always been a veil, a fiction. Now it was torn, and the question was no longer whether Muggles knew, but how they would react to what they knew.

# 

# “Containment won’t work,” she said, each syllable like stepping onto thinner ice. “Not for Harry. Not for any of us. You cannot build a cage big enough for magic. You can only work with it, or drown in the attempt.”

# 

# Laughter rang from the gallery, harsh and thin. “Then perhaps we build better cages,” someone muttered, and the words rippled outward like contagion.

# 

# The Speaker banged his gavel for order. It sounded like a toy in the chaos.

# 

# Kingsley’s shoulders shifted, just enough to angle toward Hermione, as if shielding her from the force of the chamber. “We are not here to barter chains,” he said, voice deep enough to hush the nearer benches. “We are here to discuss cooperation. This country cannot afford panic. Nor can ours. We need hours, not hostilities.”

# 

# An MP shouted, “And what will you do with those hours? Groom him into your next dictator?”

# 

# Hermione’s chest constricted. She thought of Harry on the bridge, hands trembling, eyes hollowed by the cost of pulling light through his bones. She thought of the bus teetering on the dome’s curve, the screams, the glass singing like bells. She wanted to scream herself, but instead she forced calm into her words.

# 

# “He isn’t a dictator,” she said. “He’s a man who has carried the weight of your safety for a decade. If you make him your enemy now, you will create the thing you fear.”

# 

# That silenced them more than any gavel. The words were too close to prophecy.

# 

# Hermione sank back onto the bench, her pulse like drumfire in her throat. Kingsley resumed speaking, measured, deliberate, but she barely heard him. Her eyes caught on the reporters above, their quills scratching faster than ever.

# 

# No matter what was said in this chamber, the world outside had already made its decision. Headlines were being written in real time. Dark Lord, savior, god, monster. The truth didn’t matter anymore—only the story.

# 

# When the clerk slipped Kingsley a folded note, Hermione saw the way his hand hesitated before passing it to her. She opened it, read the words, and felt her stomach drop.

# 

# ICW delegation arriving in four hours. Emergency session. Demanding Potter’s surrender.

# 

# She folded the note into neat, precise squares, as though order in paper might keep the chaos at bay. It didn’t.

Scene B: Ministry Crisis Chamber



The Ministry of Magic’s emergency chamber had been designed for secrecy, not comfort. It lay deep under Whitehall, beneath layers of wards and granite, a bunker carved when Grindelwald’s shadow stretched across Europe. Hermione had read about it once in a file she wasn’t supposed to see—how the chamber was meant to survive even if London above it fell. Tonight, it was simply drowning in people.



Dozens of witches and wizards crowded the long oak table, their robes creased from apparitions that had pulled them from beds. The air buzzed with static from spells layered hastily on spells: shielding charms, dampeners, scribal enchantments gone half-feral from overuse. Every Floo grate along the wall roared with green fire, faces appearing and vanishing in a constant churn. The babble of French, German, and Spanish mingled with the clipped tones of English clerks, a chorus of panic.



Hermione paused in the doorway, absorbing it. The noise was louder than any battlefield she remembered. Fear had its own volume.



“Granger!”



Percy Weasley shouldered through the throng like a man convinced of his own authority. His spectacles were askew, tie half-undone, but his voice cracked with bureaucratic certainty. He thrust a stack of parchments at her.



“Damage control draft,” he said. “The ICW wants Potter restrained, the Prime Minister wants Potter restrained, and Kingsley’s trying to stall both. Read it. Fix it. Sign it.”



She scanned the top sheet. The words stabbed back: containment protocols, magical sanctions, joint taskforce authority. Every line was a noose waiting to tighten.



“Absolutely not,” she said, too quickly.



Percy’s mouth went thin. “You don’t get to veto reality, Hermione. I was in the chamber. I saw what he did. The world saw what he did. If we don’t offer Potter’s leash, the ICW will demand his head.”



Hermione dropped the parchments on the table. “You’re not leashing Harry. He saved lives.”



“He endangered them first!” Percy’s voice rose. Several heads turned. “He played god with half of London. You think the public will thank him for catching a bridge they watched him nearly break?”



Hermione took a step closer, low-voiced now, sharp enough to cut. “I think the public already knows Harry better than you do. They won’t swallow spin, Percy. They saw him save them. If we sell him out now, they’ll turn on us, not him.”



“Us?” Percy echoed, bitter. “Do you hear yourself? You speak as though he’s above the Ministry. Above the ICW. Above the bloody law. That is exactly why they’re afraid.”



A burst of green flame cut him off—an embassy contact screaming about riots in Paris, footage of broomstick duels bleeding across television. Another flare: the German Minister calling the Thames dome “a declaration of magical sovereignty.” Each report slammed like another hammer on a wall already cracking.



Hermione pressed her palm against her temple. The whole world was treating Harry as a nation, and the Ministry as his embassy. That was a position no document could contain.



She moved through the crowd toward Kingsley. He stood at the far end of the table, spine straight despite the weight of two governments on his shoulders. His eyes met hers, weary and steady.



“They want a signature,” she said.



“I know,” he replied. His voice was low enough for her ears alone. “They want Potter in chains, wrapped up in ribbon so the Muggles feel safe. They don’t understand he won’t fit in any box they build.”



Hermione swallowed. “What’s the plan?”



Kingsley exhaled slowly. “Survive until dawn. Then we see if the ICW’s appetite for blood is louder than Westminster’s appetite for control.”



Behind them, Percy barked at a clerk to redraft paragraph three. His voice carried, insistent: “We present Potter as dangerous but cooperative. Sell them the leash before they forge their own.”



Hermione’s nails bit into her palms. It was always easier for bureaucrats to write chains on parchment than to carry them into the field. Percy had never been on a battlefield with Harry. He had never watched him bleed for people who whispered monster behind his back.



A Floo crackled open beside her, and the gaunt face of an American delegate filled the grate. “Our President will require technical briefings on Mr Potter’s… abilities. You will provide them, or we will provide containment.”



Hermione crouched closer to the flame, teeth bared. “You don’t contain Harry Potter,” she said. “You cooperate or you collapse.”



The delegate’s eyes flickered, uneasy, before the flame swallowed him whole.



Hermione straightened. Her heart thundered. She knew the room had heard her, and she didn’t care.



Kingsley placed a hand on her shoulder, a quiet anchor. “Careful,” he murmured. “Every word spoken here will be repeated tenfold before morning.”



“I know,” she said.



And still she thought of Harry walking alone somewhere in the city above, the light still humming in his bones. They were writing his story without him, and every page bent toward betrayal.

Scene C: Dawn Fallout



By the time Hermione stepped out of Westminster, the January sky had begun to pale. Dawn crept along the Thames with a thin, colourless light, the kind that made everything look half-finished. London was awake far earlier than it should have been, and the city felt restless, like a child who had overheard too much in the night.



The air was sharp with cold and the burnt-metal tang of exhaust. Sirens wailed somewhere distant. On every corner, papers hit the stands, and hawkers shouted headlines with voices cracked by lack of sleep. Hermione didn’t need to buy one—every discarded sheet already plastered the pavements, damp with frost.



BOY WHO LIVED OR DARK LORD REBORN?

BRIDGE OF LIGHT STUNS WORLD

MAGIC COVER-UP COLLAPSES



Every masthead screamed some variation of the same story. Some had captured still frames from phones: Harry on the Embankment, wand raised; the aureate dome blazing behind him; glass frozen mid-song in the air. The images looked like holy cards, icons torn from a cathedral.



Hermione wrapped her cloak tighter and forced herself to walk. Every face she passed seemed to be speaking of him—whispers over coffee carts, bus drivers craning to radios, commuters clustered around glowing screens. Half fearful, half reverent, all hungry.



She ducked into a café on Whitehall, more for the warmth than the tea. Inside, it was packed—soldiers in fatigues elbow to elbow with civil servants, their badges still clipped to coats. Two men argued at the counter:



“He saved the bridge, I saw it—”

“—he caused it, you daft sod. You don’t tear up London to prove you’re a hero.”



The barista rolled her eyes and shoved another cup across the counter. Hermione slipped into a corner seat, tugging a paper from the rack.



The Daily Prophet was worse. Wizard photographs looped endlessly: the dome rising, Harry’s silhouette rimmed with gold, his hand trembling on the rail. The headline slashed across the page:



THE LAST HERO, OR THE FIRST DARK LORD OF A NEW AGE?



Her stomach twisted. They always did this. When Harry won, they adored him until he refused their leash. When he faltered, they sharpened their knives. She had warned him this would happen, warned him the world would turn the moment they remembered fear.



A cough drew her attention. An elderly man at the next table had spread The Times open across his knees. His finger traced the text as he muttered aloud:



“…Ministry representative insists situation under control… opposition MPs call for oversight committee… leaked ICW memo demands Potter’s custody…”



The man shook his head. “Bloody fools. If he wanted us dead, we’d be dead. But they’ll poke him until he gives them a reason.”



Hermione wanted to thank him, wanted to tell him he was right. Instead she drained the last of her tea, bitter and lukewarm, and left.



Outside, the city had taken on the strange rhythm of crisis. Lorries idled in clogged traffic; helicopters thudded low overhead; soldiers stood at intersections with rifles they didn’t know would be useless. Screens blared newsfeeds in shopfronts: experts debating whether the dome was “coherent plasma” or “mass hallucination.” One anchor, red-eyed from the night, announced that the Prime Minister would be addressing the nation by noon.



Hermione crossed Westminster Bridge. The Thames below still looked bruised, the water sluggish where the dome had bent it. Tourists clustered at the railings, phones held out like offerings. One woman touched the stone balustrade reverently, as if expecting it to sing again.



Hermione’s reflection stared back from the water: tired eyes, hair frizzed from rain and smoke, shoulders tight with the weight of lies. She pressed her palm against the stone. It was solid, ordinary, utterly unmoved by the night before. And yet she could almost hear it, a ghost of resonance still trembling in her bones.



How long before they decide you’re as dangerous as he is? whispered a treacherous voice in her mind. She shoved it down.



She thought of Harry somewhere in this vast, unsettled city, walking alone. Was he hiding? Planning? Or simply trying to breathe without the glass singing in his blood? She longed to find him, to anchor him, but she knew better. He needed space—or perhaps he needed to realise just how quickly the world was closing in.



By the time she returned to the Ministry, the sun had cleared the horizon. The atrium was chaos: reporters pressing against wards, owls dive-bombing with contradictory dispatches, junior clerks sprinting from office to office. The great fountain of wizardkind splashed heedless of the frenzy, its bronze statues gazing upward as though they knew nothing of panic.



Percy intercepted her before she reached Kingsley’s office. He looked like he hadn’t slept in weeks, though it had only been hours. “The ICW’s delegation is already at Heathrow. They’ll be here by midday.” He adjusted his spectacles with a shaking hand. “If Kingsley won’t leash Potter, they will. And if they fail, the Muggles will try instead. Which do you prefer?”



Hermione met his eyes. “Neither. Because both end with war.”



“Then perhaps war is inevitable,” Percy snapped. “Better we choose the field than let Potter choose it for us.”



She brushed past him, unwilling to waste another breath. But the echo of his words followed her into the lift, into the rising hum of the Ministry, into the cold certainty forming in her chest.



Perhaps war is inevitable.

Scene D: Press Confrontation



The iron gates outside Westminster were supposed to be guarded, but by mid-morning they had become a stage. Reporters pressed shoulder to shoulder against the railings, their cameras and enchanted quills raised like weapons. Beyond them, citizens clustered in swelling circles, hungry for scraps of truth.



Hermione stepped out into the pale daylight with her head down, but the crowd surged as if they had scented blood.



“Miss Granger!” a voice shouted. “Is it true Potter conjured a weapon of mass destruction over the Thames?”



“Is he cooperating with the Ministry?” another barked.



“Will he face trial?”



The questions piled, sharp and relentless. Hermione froze for half a heartbeat, her instincts screaming for wand and shield. But wandwork here would undo every careful stitch Kingsley had tried to sew. She forced her jaw to unclench, raised both hands—not in surrender, but in command.



“I will speak,” she said, projecting her voice the way McGonagall had taught her years ago. It cut through the clamour like a blade. “But you will listen.”



The crowd hushed, if only from shock.



Hermione drew a breath. Every word mattered now. “What happened last night was not an attack. The bridge was failing. Harry Potter caught it. You may debate his methods, but you cannot deny his intent. He saved lives.”



“Saved them by endangering them!” a tabloid man jeered, shoving a microphone forward. “What if the dome had collapsed? What if it happens again?”



Hermione fixed him with a stare that had once silenced Death Eaters. “What if he hadn’t been there? How many coffins would you be printing on your front page this morning?”



A ripple moved through the press pack. Some lowered their eyes. Others scribbled faster.



A witch from the Daily Prophet thrust her quill at Hermione. “The ICW demands Potter’s surrender. Will the Ministry comply?”



The words struck like a blow, but Hermione did not flinch. “The Ministry is not in the business of sacrificing its saviours to appease fear.”



“Then you admit he’s out of control?”



“I admit he is powerful,” Hermione shot back. “And power must be guided, not shackled. Harry Potter has guided this world through worse storms than you can imagine. If you make him your enemy now, you will write the prophecy you fear.”



Gasps, furious scribbling, the rustle of cameras.



From the crowd beyond the press, a woman’s voice rang out: “He saved us! I was on that bridge!”



The reporters turned, hungry for her face, but the woman ducked away, swallowed by the bodies. Hermione felt a flicker of gratitude—one honest voice, however fleeting, might yet shift the tide.



A younger journalist edged forward, his notepad trembling in his hands. “Miss Granger, with respect… are you saying Potter is beyond the law?”



Hermione hesitated. The truth was jagged, and any edge she offered could cut them all. “I am saying,” she answered carefully, “that laws must adapt to realities. Magic cannot be hidden any longer. We either write a new chapter together, or we let fear write it for us.”



The murmur that followed was not quite applause, not quite dissent. It was something heavier: acknowledgement that the world had already changed, and no gavel or decree could make it small again.



Flashes erupted, capturing her face in harsh bursts of light. She wondered how the Prophet would twist her words by noon, how Parliament would seize them, how the ICW would spit them back across the Channel. She wondered what Harry would think, reading his fate written in headlines instead of history.



A firm hand touched her shoulder. Kingsley had arrived, flanked by Aurors in plain robes. He leaned close, his voice low enough for her ears alone. “That was either brilliance or disaster,” he murmured. “Time will tell which.”



Hermione exhaled slowly, lowering her hands. The press roared again, but the Aurors closed ranks, ushering her back toward the doors.



As she crossed the threshold, Hermione caught her reflection in the glass: tired, defiant, carrying more weight than one witch should. The city behind her buzzed with anger and awe, its heartbeat quickening toward something inevitable.



She thought of Harry, somewhere out there, carrying a burden ten times heavier. The world was already shaping him into what it feared. And if they weren’t careful—if she wasn’t careful—it would succeed.
